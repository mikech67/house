This simulation lets you experiment with house construction to see how your insulation choices affect a home's energy consumption. You'll find that proper insulation can dramatically reduce the amount of power needed to keep a house comfortably warm in cold weather.

Start by choosing the size of your house. There are red circles on three of the house's corners and dragging those circles with the mouse changes the house's length, width, and height. Once the house size is set, adjust the house's window area by dragging the circles on its windows.

Next, set the indoor and outdoor temperatures. You can enter these values in either °C or °F. The colder the outdoor temperature and the warmer the indoor temperature, the faster heat will flow out of the house through its walls, ceiling, and windows. You can choose temperatures from -40 °C to +40 °C, although the indoor temperature can't be less than the outdoor temperature.

With these basic settings complete, it's time to insulate your house. Your insulation choices for the walls and ceilings are brick, wood, fiberglass, and urethane foam. The thermal characteristics of those materials are:

Material Thermal conductivity Approximate US R-value
Brick 0.60 W/m·K R-0.1 cm-1 (R-0.25 in-1)
Wood 0.15 W/m·K R-0.4 cm-1 (R-1.0 in-1)
Fiberglass 0.040 W/m·K R-1.4 cm-1 (R-3.5 in-1)
Urethane Foam 0.021 W/m·K R-2.7 cm-1 (R-6.8 in-1)

In addition to picking the type of insulation, you can adjust its thickness. That thickness can range from 2 to 500 cm. Below 2 cm, your house wouldn't have a real wall or ceiling. Above 500 cm, its insulation would be impractically thick. The thicker the insulation and the smaller its thermal conductivity, the less heat will flow through each square-meter of it. For practical reasons, you can't use brick for the ceiling insulation.

Windows are also surprisingly important when it comes to keeping your house warm. You can't choose the thickness of your windows, but you can choose their construction: single pane, double pane, Low-E double pane, and Low-E argon-filled double pane. The thermal characteristics of those windows are:

Type Thermal conduction Approximate US R-value
Single pane 6.7 W/m2·K R-0.85
Double pane 2.8 W/m2·K R-2.0
Low-E double pane 1.9 W/m2·K R-3.0
Low-E argon-filled 1.6 W/m2·K R-3.6

As you assemble and insulate your house, watch how much flows through its walls, ceiling, and windows. With poor insulation, a surprising amount of thermal power escapes from your house and your furnace or woodstove will have to supply that amount of thermal power to keep your house at the indoor temperature you selected.

Make adjustments to your house size or insulation, or change the indoor or outdoor temperatures. With reasonable insulating choices, you'll find that you can make an enormous difference in the amount of power needed to heat your home. You can also make surprisingly bad choices, so be glad that you're only working on with simulation.
